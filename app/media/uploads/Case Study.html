01 · The premise

Adult friendships die by scheduling.

Most of the people we love most live within thirty minutes of us. We see them less than we'd like — not because we're busy, but because seeing them takes a calendar, two reschedules, and a restaurant booking. The threshold is too high for "I'd just like to sit with you."

Sponte makes "I'm around if anyone wants to swing by" a single tap. No invitations. No replies expected. No event. Just a lantern in the window, on or off, for the people you already trust.

Calendars are for strangers. Friends should know where the porch light is.

Everything in the app comes back to one decision: is your lantern lit, and for whom? The product is the answer to that question, surfaced wherever you happen to look — your phone, your wrist, your home screen, your laptop.

02 · The metaphor

A lantern, not a status.

"Free now / busy / DND" reads like a slack indicator. A lantern reads like a place. Concrete, warm, already understood. Every interaction in the app is a thing you can do to a lantern — light it, dim it, leave it on a timer, let the right people see it.

i.

Lit, dim, or on a timer.

Tap to light. The lantern stays on until a time you set — 30 min, an hour, until dinner. You don't have to remember to dim it; it ages out on its own.

ii.

For a circle, not the public.

Only people you've added can see your lantern. Within that, you choose: everyone, a group, or specific names. Privacy is the default, not a setting.

iii.

With an optional ask.

"Bring something" and "mood" are tiny add-ons — a bottle of wine, a book to read in the same room. They're optional because most lanterns don't need them.

03 · The magic moment

One sheet. Light the lantern.

The hardest design decision was resisting a wizard. Audience, duration, mood, "bring something" — every one of these could justify its own screen. None of them earn one. They live as a single sheet that opens from the home tab, defaults you can ignore, and ships in one swipe.

The fast path is two taps: light → done. Everything else is there if you want it, invisible if you don't. defaults do the work →

04 · Variant exploration

Three ways to set the time.

Duration is the one input you'll touch every time. I prototyped three. Each is honest about a different posture: pick a preset, sweep to a feeling, or commit to a precise hour. The dial shipped as default; chips and slider stay as adjustable settings for users who reach for them.

Chips

Fastest

Five presets — 30m, 1h, 2h, "til 6", "tonight." Zero precision, total speed. Best when you've barely thought about it.

Dial

Default

A radial dial that snaps to the next quarter-hour. Feels like setting an alarm clock — a deliberate, calming gesture that matches the lantern itself.

Slider

Precise

A horizontal sweep from "now" to "tonight." Continuous and analog. Best for people who think of time as a feeling rather than a clock.

05 · Range

Same lantern, every surface.

A circle isn't on one device. Some people live in a browser; some never leave the wrist. The same home state — your lantern, your friends' lanterns — renders cleanly on phone, tablet, and web, with each surface earning the space it gets.

06 · Ambient surfaces

Out of the way, still on.

A lantern is something you glance at. It doesn't deserve a notification every time someone's around. Sponte lives where glances already happen — a Live Activity on the lock screen, a Watch complication on your wrist, a home-screen widget you scan on the way out the door.

Open the app to change your state. The rest of the time, your state changes you — a soft warm glow on whatever you happen to be looking at.

07 · Visual system

Cream, coral, candlelight.

The palette is a porch at dusk. Warm cream as paper, charcoal as ink, coral as the lantern, honey as the glow. Greys are tinted toward brown so nothing feels like a spreadsheet. A serif (Newsreader) carries the brand voice; a humanist sans (Manrope) carries the interface; a handwritten accent (Caveat) shows up as marginalia.

08 · Reflection

What I'd test next.

The default duration. A dial that lands on 1h feels right to me; data might say 30m is the honest number. The first version I'd ship instruments this hard.

The receiving side. I designed for the lighter more than the seer. I'd next prototype the "who's free" surface as a separate ambient widget — maybe a tiny constellation of lit windows on the home screen — and see if people open the app less.

The first lantern. Onboarding has a real ceiling: there's no value until someone in your contacts is also on Sponte. The current design leans into the "invite a friend, light a lantern together" loop. Whether that loop is strong enough is the only question that matters before launch.
